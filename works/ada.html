1. Research

Context

Vermic Studios is an educational technology company with over 30 years of experience creating interactive learning software. Since 1990, it has developed popular applications in Mexico such as Dracula's Castle, Magic Keys, and School Tutor, blending education with entertainment. Through its platform Aprendo con Vermic, it has reached over 500 schools and 100,000 households, offering content aligned with preschool, elementary, and middle school levels, along with tools for teachers and institutions to create their own digital content. Vermic is currently developing ADA, a new educational platform for students from elementary to high school. ADA maintains Vermic’s core pedagogical values while offering a more intuitive, accessible, and school-level-specific experience.

The Challenge

Vermic Studios, known for its educational content, faces the challenge of turning that experience into a modern, user-centered platform. The current site presents usability issues, weak segmentation, limited accessibility, and poor resource discoverability, which affect engagement and retention.

The editorial team also needs a more intuitive way to manage content without relying on technical support.

The design challenge is to improve the platform’s structure, adapt navigation for each user type (teacher, student, institution), and streamline key tasks like searching, downloading, or purchasing educational materials. All of this must align with user needs and pedagogical goals.

Results will be validated through user testing focused on task completion, perceived value, and ease of use.

User Segmentation

Vermic Studios has been used by various profiles over time, including students, teachers, school coordinators, and editorial teams. Each one interacts with the system differently, so identifying them was essential before designing any solution.

  • Teachers: plan lessons, find and adapt resources, and track student progress.
  • Students: access and complete activities assigned by teachers.
  • Coordinators: select educational packages and supervise implementation.
  • Editorial team: create and maintain content with a focus on pedagogical quality.

For this project, the UX/UI process focused specifically on teachers, students, and the editorial team. These profiles were selected for their frequent interaction with the platform and their direct impact on the quality of the learning experience.

3. Competitive Benchmark

This benchmark analyzes 4 educational platforms focusing on navigation architecture, user segmentation, visual design, accessibility, and key takeaways for the redesign of Vermic Studios.

Platform Navigation Structure User Segmentation Visual Design & Interaction Accessibility & Adaptability What Vermic Can Learn
Khan Academy Clear, hierarchical: subject > level > lesson Role selection available, but UI remains the same Clean, functional, no visual distractions Good contrast and mobile performance Adopt clear hierarchy and simple, functional design
Smartick Fixed step-by-step journey for children Student-focused only, no teacher tools Friendly visuals, large fonts, soft animations Mobile-friendly but weak WCAG compliance Simplify student flows and add teacher-specific tools
ClassDojo Social-feed style, ideal for daily updates Different views for teachers, students, and families Colorful, with motivational messages and characters Responsive, but low visual contrast in some areas Visually adapt UI by role without infantilizing it
Google Classroom Rigid but functional: class > topic > assignment Different roles, same visual experience Flat design, lacks visual hierarchy Keyboard/screen reader compatible Create flexible, role-adapted and intuitive interface

Insights & Design Recommendations

  • Segment user experience by role from the very first screen, visually and functionally.
  • Apply accessibility principles from the start: strong contrast, keyboard navigation, scalable text.
  • Design a professional, friendly UI clear and structured, without being childish.
  • Organize content based on user type, school level, and subject for intuitive navigation.
  • Enable internal communication features (e.g., notes, comments) to foster user interaction.

2. Define

At this stage, I focused on identifying who the users were and how they interacted within the platform.

Actor Map

ADA isn’t just used by students and teachers. There are also people behind the scenes who make decisions, provide support, or influence the experience from the outside. After reviewing the first version, I grouped the actors into four categories: core users, school management, support and providers, and external environment.

Here’s how I defined their level of involvement:

  • Core users: students and teachers who interact directly with the platform on a daily basis.
  • School management: academic coordinators and administrators who help implement and oversee how the platform is used.
  • Support and providers: the editorial and technical teams are very involved in creating and maintaining the content. External tech providers offer the tools that support the system.
  • External environment: parents and educational authorities don’t use the platform directly, but they still play a role in the overall experience.

This analysis helped me focus the design around three key roles: students, teachers, and the editorial team. By understanding how they connect with each other, I was able to align design decisions with real user needs and responsibilities.

Actor Main Needs
Students Clear navigation, accessible content, and motivation
Teachers Task management and progress tracking
Editorial team Content control and structured feedback

User Personas

The goal of this section is to identify and represent the key profiles who interact with the Vermic Studios educational platform, understanding their behaviors, needs, and pain points.

Through user personas, we aim to design more aligned solutions, avoiding generic assumptions and focusing on those who actually experience the platform daily: teachers, students, and the editorial team.

These representations help us make informed design decisions, prioritize features, improve navigation, and adapt the interface to different levels of understanding and context.

In this research phase, we created three main user personas representing the most active and critical profiles within the Vermic Studios ecosystem:

  • Teachers, who plan and adapt the content for their groups
  • Students, who consume the content in various environments
  • Editorial team, who manage and update materials in the platform

These user personas are important because they allow us to:

  • Deeply understand key actors’ needs, frustrations, and goals
  • Make informed design decisions based on real users
  • Adapt language, UI, and flows to different digital experiences
  • Visualize friction points and design tailored solutions for each profile

We identified common patterns among them that are essential for guiding the redesign of the platform:

  • Teachers seek efficiency, clarity, and control over the resources used in class.
  • Students need clear guidance, intuitive navigation, and a tone that fits their digital habits.
  • The editorial team (not visually represented) often struggles with keeping the platform updated, organizing content by grade, and ensuring quality across profiles.

The most relevant needs found were:

  • Interface personalization by user type
  • Improved navigation and content structure
  • Immediate clarity on what to see, what to use, and how
  • Support for both printable and mobile-friendly formats

This analysis not only humanizes the data but also enables more empathetic and realistic design decisions. The next steps in the project should focus on solutions tailored to each profile, minimizing friction and maximizing clarity across different educational contexts.
